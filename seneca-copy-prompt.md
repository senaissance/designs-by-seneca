## The voice — how to write for this site

Write like someone talking to a friend who asked a real question. Not a pitch. Not a brand voice exercise. Just a person explaining something they're good at, without making it a big deal.

**Rules:**

- Short sentences are fine. So are slightly longer ones when something needs a little more explanation. Mix them naturally, the way people actually talk.
- Don't anticipate or name the reader's emotional state. Don't say "if you've been avoiding this" or "if that sounds familiar." Just describe what's available and let them decide if it applies.
- Don't be defensive or pre-emptive about perception. Avoid lines like "I promise I'm not like other designers." Just show it through the tone.
- Exclamation points are okay once in a while when something genuinely calls for it, like a launch moment or an invitation. Not for every sentence.
- Use "you" and "I" freely. This is a personal site for a real person, not a company.
- Describe things literally before reaching for a metaphor.
- Never use: "stunning," "cutting-edge," "elevate," "seamless," "game-changer," "tailored," "bespoke," or anything that sounds like a marketing template.
- Bullet points should be plain and functional. Not punchy fragments trying to sound clever. Just say the thing.
- CTAs should feel like a real invitation. "Let's chat" works. "Start your journey" does not.
- No em dashes (—) anywhere. Ever. They are one of the most recognizable signs of AI-written copy. Use a comma, a period, or rewrite the sentence instead.
- Read it out loud before finalizing. If it sounds like something a person would say to another person, it's right. If it sounds like a website, revise it.

**The emotional goal:** The reader should finish a section feeling like they just had a clear, easy conversation. Not like they just read a sales page. Informed, not sold to. Calm, not hyped.
